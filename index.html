The Case for Urban Mining

Rare earth elements (REEs) are indispensable to virtually every clean-energy and advanced-electronics system. Yet fewer than 2% of REEs in end-of-life products are currently recovered. With China imposing export licensing requirements on neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, and terbium in April 2025, and expanding controls to five additional elements in October 2025, the urgency to develop secondary supply chains has become acute.

This report examines five distinct secondary source streams: (1) tin recovered from tin-coated copper scrap, (2) neodymium from hard disk drives and consumer electronics, (3) neodymium, dysprosium, and praseodymium from NdFeB permanent magnets, (4) yttrium, europium, and terbium from fluorescent lamp phosphors, and (5) lanthanum, cerium, neodymium, and praseodymium from nickel-metal hydride batteries. Each section documents extraction processes, elemental composition and yield percentages, market values, and the competitive landscape in Asia-Pacific.

Strategic Opportunity: By 2035, up to 25% of global REE demand could be met through recycling if adequate collection and processing infrastructure is established. Heavy REEs (dysprosium, terbium, yttrium) — where China's grip is tightest — represent the highest-value opportunity for secondary recovery.

The Vapour-Loss Problem in Copper Smelting

When tin-coated copper is smelted to recover copper, tin behaves in a fundamentally different way from copper. Under standard pyrometallurgical conditions, tin oxide (SnO₂) — formed from the tin coating — is reduced to metallic tin and partially dissolves into the copper-lead bullion. However, if conditions are insufficiently reducing, or temperatures surge, tin volatilises as stannous oxide (SnO) fumes and escapes in the off-gas stream. This "vapour loss" renders the tin unrecoverable in most conventional smelters.

Industrial smelters mitigate losses by operating under slightly reducing conditions and capturing furnace off-gases, but this is rarely optimised for maximum tin yield. Printed circuit boards contain on average 3% tin (as solder), representing a huge reservoir of secondary tin. Asia generates 48% of global e-waste by mass — the single largest secondary tin feedstock pool on earth.

Strategic Opportunity: The Vapour-Capture Advantage

By installing purpose-built off-gas capture systems (venturi scrubbers + baghouse filters) on existing copper smelters that process tin-coated copper, secondary tin recovery can be improved from an industry-average of ~60% to over 90%. The tin-rich oxide dust collected in bag filters can be reprocessed in a dedicated slag fuming furnace or sold to specialist tin smelters (e.g., Malaysia Smelting Corporation, Thaisarco).

Market Positioning: If a single integrated facility processes India's e-waste stream (India generates ~3.2 million tonnes/year) with optimised tin capture, recovering even 50% of the tin content from PCBs (~1.5 kg per tonne), this equates to ~2,400 tonnes of tin per year — placing it among the top-20 secondary tin producers globally at current prices (~$74M/year in tin value alone).

Key Technology Approaches

Pyrometallurgical Route
TBRC + Slag Fuming
Top-blown rotary converter under reducing gas. Tin collects in metal bullion. Slag fuming step extracts remaining Sn from SnO-rich slag using carbon reductant at 1200–1400°C. Recovery: 85–92% of tin input. Best for high-volume mixed scrap.
Hydrometallurgical Route
HCl / H₂SO₄ Leaching
Selective dissolution of tin from copper substrate using hydrochloric or sulfuric acid. Tin precipitated as SnO₂ or electrodeposited as pure metal. Recovery: 90–96% of tin. Best for low-volume, high-purity applications (food-grade tinplate recycling).

Neodymium in Hard Disk Drives

Every hard disk drive (HDD) contains one or more small NdFeB permanent magnets — typically 3–10 grams each — embedded in the voice coil actuator assembly. Despite the small mass per unit, the sheer volume of HDDs discarded globally (hundreds of millions per year as SSDs displace them) makes HDDs a significant secondary source of neodymium. A single tonne of HDD magnets contains approximately 200–300 kg of rare earth oxides.

Consumer spectacle frames made with neodymium-doped glass (to improve colour and optical clarity) and neodymium-based alloys for lightweight frames are another emerging secondary source, though the concentration is lower and collection more dispersed.

NdFeB Magnet Composition — The Feedstock Profile

NdFeB (neodymium-iron-boron) permanent magnets form the intermetallic compound Nd₂Fe₁₄B as their primary phase. They are composed of approximately 69% iron, 30% rare earth elements, and 1% boron by weight. The rare earth fraction is primarily neodymium, with praseodymium, dysprosium, and sometimes terbium added in varying quantities depending on the grade and application.

Fluorescent Lamp Phosphor — A High-Grade Secondary Ore

Fluorescent lamps (CFLs and linear tubes) are coated internally with 4–6 grams of tri-band phosphor powder. This powder contains 20–30% rare earth oxides — a concentration that exceeds even the richest primary REE ores. Three primary phosphor systems are present in each lamp: (1) the red phosphor Y₂O₃:Eu³⁺ (YOX), which is easily dissolved in dilute acid; (2) the green phosphors LaPO₄:Ce³⁺,Tb³⁺ (LAP) and (Ce,Tb)MgAl₁₁O₁₉ (CAT), which are aluminate/phosphate matrices and more resistant to acid leaching; and (3) the blue phosphor BaMgAl₁₀O₁₇:Eu²⁺ (BAM).

Approximately 25,000 tonnes of rare earth-containing waste fluorescent lamp phosphors are stockpiled worldwide. India alone produced 500 million CFL units plus 250 million fluorescent tubes between 2014–2015. The phase-out of fluorescent lamps under energy efficiency regulations creates a predictable and growing waste stream ripe for organised collection.

NiMH Battery — The Hidden REE Reservoir

Nickel-metal hydride batteries were the dominant rechargeable battery chemistry in hybrid electric vehicles (Toyota Prius, Honda Insight) and millions of consumer devices from the 1990s through the 2010s. As lithium-ion batteries continue displacing NiMH, a massive wave of spent NiMH batteries is entering the waste stream. Each HEV battery pack contains approximately 3.5 kg of rare earth elements — predominantly lanthanum, cerium, praseodymium, and neodymium in the hydrogen-absorbing anode alloy.

Despite this, over 90% of NiMH battery REEs are currently discarded in landfills or lost as oxide slag when batteries are processed in steelmaking furnaces (where REEs report to slag rather than to the valuable nickel-cobalt alloy). The current industrial approach — adding NiMH batteries to stainless steel feedstock as a cheap nickel source — recovers the nickel but wastes the REEs entirely.

Gap = Opportunity: More than 90% of REEs in end-of-life NiMH batteries are currently lost. At typical REE concentrations of 8–17 wt% and current prices for Nd ($245/kg) and Pr ($100/kg), a dedicated NiMH REE recovery line processing 10,000 tonnes of battery black mass per year could yield $18–35M in REE value alone — on top of the nickel and cobalt value.
